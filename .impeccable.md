## Design Context

### Users
Everyday consumers — not activists, not lawyers, not health researchers. People who bought shampoo and sunscreen and cleaning products without thinking twice. They're discovering, possibly for the first time, that those purchases may have exposed them to carcinogens and that real legal recourse exists. They use this app once or twice, with high emotional stakes. They're not living in a dashboard — they're going through a discovery moment.

### Brand Personality
**Vigilant. Empowering. Resourceful.**

This is a product that sees what others miss and equips ordinary people to act on it. The tone is that of a sharp, trusted advocate — not a cold medical instrument, not an ambulance-chasing law firm, not a SaaS productivity tool. Think: the firm but warm clarity of a good investigative journalist or a consumer rights attorney who actually cares about the outcome.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Reference**: Apple.com — sleek, spacious, editorial, confident. Products speak for themselves. Generous breathing room signals that something important is being said.
**Anti-reference**: Generic SaaS dashboards (tightly packed cards, neon accent stripes, every-element-the-same-size grids). Should feel like a premium consumer product, not an admin panel.

**Visual tone**: Light mode. Warm off-white surfaces. Deep, authoritative type. Minimal use of color — let the data and the action hierarchy carry visual weight. One strong accent that signals urgency only when something truly warrants it.

**Typography direction**: Large, confident display type for key moments (score reveal, lawsuit count). Clean, highly legible body type for data and descriptions. Not techy-monospace. Not editorial-serif. A geometric sans with personality that reads as considered and trustworthy, not clinical.

**Color direction**: Move away from the generic teal-on-white SaaS look. Consider a deep slate-green or near-black as a primary authority color, with a warm cream/ivory background (not pure white). A single vivid accent reserved only for calls-to-action and critical risk signals — not scattered across the UI.

### Design Principles

1. **Evidence over alarm** — Show data with authority, not panic. The interface should feel like a trusted advisor delivering important facts, not a tabloid headline. Urgency comes from the content, not from visual noise.

2. **Breathing room signals trust** — Spacious layouts feel considered and premium for a consumer encountering something this important. Tight, dense dashboards feel like they're hiding something or rushing you past the details.

3. **Hierarchy always points toward action** — Every screen should have one dominant next step. The visual weight should guide users from discovery → understanding → action, not leave them paralyzed by equal-weight options.

4. **Consumer polish, not analyst utility** — This product is used by people in a high-stakes discovery moment, not by experts who live in it. Every element should feel approachable and clear to someone with no background in chemistry or law.

5. **Earned moments of emphasis** — Because breathing room and restraint are the default, the moments where color, size, or weight is dramatically increased carry real meaning. A "critical risk" label that uses the same visual language as everything else means nothing. Reserve strong visual signals for genuinely important signals.
